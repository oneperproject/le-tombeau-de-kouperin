This sensibility is deeply rooted in classical literature and has been noted by many critics: Classical Example – Heike Monogatari: The 13th-century epic Tale of the Heike opens with the famous lines about the impermanence of worldly glory, and it “narrates the Heike clan’s downfall, finding beauty in their ruin”elabo-mag.com. This tragic elegance (儚さの美) reflects the Buddhist notion of mujo (impermanence). The tale, recited by biwa troubadours, aestheticized defeat as something poignant and memorableelabo-mag.com. Such appreciation of ephemeral glory (the fall of the mighty) is often cited as a distinctly Japanese sensibility. Impermanence and “Mono no Aware”: Japanese aesthetic thought traditionally embraces mono no aware (the pathos of things) – an awareness of impermanence that finds beauty in transient moments and endings. Critics like Kobayashi Hideo and Karaki Junzō have extensively discussed Japan’s “sense of mujo (impermanence)”, emphasizing how literature cherishes mortality and declineosaka-geidai.ac.jp. For example, Kobayashi’s essay “Mujō to iu koto” (1946) and Karaki’s “Mujo” (1964) analyze how works from medieval war tales to haikai poetry incorporate an aesthetic of lossosaka-geidai.ac.jp. In short, there is ample precedent for valuing the beauty of a graceful demise in Japanese literary criticism. Modern Literature – Dazai and Mishima: In the 20th century, authors like Dazai Osamu and Mishima Yukio carried on this ruin aesthetic in their lives and writings. Both writers cultivated themes of decadence, failure, and dramatic death. In fact, a 1992 study “滅びの美学: 太宰治と三島由紀夫” (Aesthetics of Ruin: Dazai Osamu and Mishima Yukio) explicitly analyzes how these two embodied the beauty of ruin in modern literaturebookmeter.com. Mishima’s fascination with heroic death and Dazai’s melancholic losers illustrate a continued critical interest in the “beauty of defeat.” Critics have noted that underlying both authors’ works is an attraction to hōrobi no bi (the beauty in destruction), linking them to that older traditionvaluebooks.jp. In summary, finding an aesthetic or “beauty” in narratives of defeat is not a new idea – it runs from medieval war tales to postwar literary criticism. The “beauty of defeat” or “aesthetics of collapse” has been a recurring critical stance in Japan’s cultural commentaryelabo-mag.comosaka-geidai.ac.jp. Language as a Device for Spirit and Memory There are also precedents for arguing that the Japanese language itself serves as a vessel of spirit or a repository of collective memory. Several scholars and ideologues (across linguistics, folklore, and criticism) have made points similar to this view: “Language as the Carrier of Spirit”: Early 20th-century linguist Ueda Kazutoshi famously declared “日本語ハ日本人ノ精神的血液ナリ” – “The Japanese language is the spiritual blood of the Japanese people.”segawa.matrix.jp. This statement (from 1903) explicitly ties the form of the Japanese language to the national spirit, suggesting that the language preserves and circulates the people’s psyche. This idea, often termed the “Japanese language = Japanese spirit” theory, posits that linguistic form is inseparable from cultural soulsegawa.matrix.jp. During the 1930s–40s, such views were influential: for example, a 1937 education ordinance in Japanese-occupied Manchuria insisted that teaching Japanese must impart Japanese spirit and customs through the languagesegawa.matrix.jp. In other words, policy-makers believed the Japanese language functioned as a “spiritual training” and cultural memory apparatus for those who learned it. Kotodama (Word-Spirit): The ancient Shintō concept of 言霊 (kotodama) also illustrates the belief that language can house spiritual essence. Kotodama is the “myth of the spirit of language” – the idea that a divine power resides in words (especially the Japanese language)blog.oup.com. In this belief, words are not merely communication tools but vessels of spiritual force, capable of preserving and releasing spiritual energy. As one analysis notes, kotodama assumes that “language has a soul”, and this notion survived throughout Japanese history in various formsblog.oup.comblog.oup.com. The prevalence of kotodama in cultural discourse (even into the modern era) shows a longstanding view of Japanese language as a “spirit container”, an almost literal device for preserving spiritual essence through verbal form. Language and Historical Memory (Oral Narrative): Another angle is the role of Japanese language in preserving historical memories via narrative. The oral storytelling tradition is strong – as seen in how Heike Monogatari was passed down by generations of storytellers (琵琶法師) to keep the memory of the Heike clan’s fall aliveelabo-mag.com. In modern times, this continues in practices like the 語り部 (kataribe), storytellers who verbally transmit memories of events (for example, Hiroshima and WWII survivors who recount their experiences so that the history is not forgotten). Such narrative practices implicitly rely on the Japanese language as a “memory preservation device,” carrying collective trauma and lessons forward. Indeed, literary critic Kato Shūichi observed that in Japan, the history of literature reflects the history of the people’s thought and sensibility – implying literature (in the Japanese language) serves as an archive of the Japanese spirit and historical experiencekshu.web.fc2.com. In short, there is precedent for viewing Japanese language and storytelling as tools for conserving cultural memory and identity. Modern Reflections: Contemporary writers have also touched on this theme. For instance, novelist Minae Mizumura argues that the Japanese language is a repository of a unique literary heritage that conveys the Japanese worldview. In 「日本語が亡びるとき」 (The Fall of Language in the Age of English, 2008), she stresses that losing Japanese would mean losing access to the “spirit” embedded in its literature and history. This reinforces the idea that the structure and continuity of the Japanese language safeguard historical memory (through classical texts, idioms, and cultural nuance passed down in Japanese). In sum, there are clear precedents for claiming that the Japanese language’s form functions as a “preserver of spirit” and a conduit for historical memory. From national language ideology (language as the nation’s soulsegawa.matrix.jp) to folk belief (kotodama’s spiritual power in wordsblog.oup.com) to literary criticism (language as the archive of a culture’s sensibilitykshu.web.fc2.com), the notion of language as a living storehouse of a people’s spirit and collective memory has been well documented. Prosperity vs. Ruin: Postwar Values in Contrast Several writers have also explicitly contrasted American-style “prosperity” narratives with Japanese “ruin/defeat” narratives, especially in the post-WWII context. The postwar period in Japan saw an influx of American values – optimism about progress, democracy, and material success – which stood in contrast to an older ethos that found meaning (even beauty) in defeat and continuity with the past. Notable discussions include: American “Success” Narrative After WWII: During the Occupation, Americans actively promoted a forward-looking culture of “peace and democracy” tied to prosperity. Historian John W. Dower notes that after 1945, American ideals of progress were widely embraced by Japanese societyweb.mit.edu. The U.S. introduced institutions and ideals for rebuilding: economic growth, individualism, and a positive future-oriented outlook replaced the old militarist ethos. This “updated form of prosperity” was largely accepted as Japan rebuilt itself. Dower’s aptly titled book Embracing Defeat (1999) suggests the Japanese not only endured defeat but in many ways found renewal by adopting the victors’ narrative of success. As he observed, much of the populace eagerly absorbed the “bright and constructive” postwar culture that emphasized moving on and thrivingweb.mit.edu. Persistence of the Aesthetics of Defeat: Despite the Americanization of values, some Japanese intellectuals and artists clung to the narrative of defeat as a source of identity and beauty. The “aesthetics of ruin” did not vanish in 1945. For example, cultural critic Takeuchi Yoshimi argued that truly processing Japan’s situation required remembering the defeat rather than simply erasing it. He warned that “defeat becomes definitive only by forgetting the fact of defeat,” and urged resistance against forgettingeaa.c.u-tokyo.ac.jp. Takeuchi’s stance (in essays like 「日本とアジア」 and 「敗北を忘れることへの抵抗」) was essentially a critique of uncritical Americanization – he believed Japan should embrace the memory of its defeat (敗戦) as a lesson and a form of cultural resistance, instead of just celebrating the American-style victory narrative. This created a dialectic between prosperity and ruin: the mainstream focused on prosperity and “winning” in the new order, while a countercurrent of thought valued the humbled, introspective perspective of the defeated. Cultural Dichotomy: Some scholars frame this contrast in terms of differing temporal or moral orientations. For instance, it is often said that Western (American) culture idealizes linear progress and triumph, whereas Japanese culture (inflected by Buddhism and cyclic time) is more accepting of cyclical rise-and-fallosaka-geidai.ac.jp. One analysis notes that Western narratives tend to be future-driven, while in Japan the repetition of seasons and history fostered an awareness of subtle loss and change – a sensitivity to impermanence (無常) that values what passes awayosaka-geidai.ac.jp. Thus, after the war, American-style “bright future” rhetoric resonated on one level, but emotionally Japan maintained a connection to its past and its pains. Commentators have pointed out that even with modern education, “Japanese people still emotionally love this kind of noble demise”, the very 滅びの美学 (aesthetics of ruin) apparent in historical narrativescompass-point.jp. In other words, the defeatist beauty ethos persisted underneath newfound prosperity. Modern Reflections in Media: The tension between prosperity and ruin as narratives surfaces in literature and film as well. Postwar writers like Ōe Kenzaburō and Mishima Yukio explored themes of loss, identity crisis, and the “nobility” of failure in the shadow of Japan’s economic miracle. More recently, popular culture (e.g. anime re-tellings of Heike Monogatari or movies like The Longest Day of Japan) revisit historical defeats with a kind of melancholic admiration, implicitly contrasting them with the contemporary era of peace and affluence. Sociologist Akiko Hashimoto in The Long Defeat (2015) speaks of how war memory in Japan oscillates between narratives of national pride and remorse, indicating an ongoing conversation about whether Japan’s identity is built on its postwar success or its wartime loss. This suggests that prosperity vs. ruin is a framework used to understand Japan’s postwar trajectory: materially victorious through Americanization, yet culturally still processing the legacy of defeat. In summary, there have indeed been critical discussions contrasting American-style prosperity with Japanese narratives of ruin/defeat. Postwar Japan’s experience was often described as a balancing act between embracing a new success narrative and preserving the “beautiful” memory of a lost war. From John Dower’s historical analysis (how Japan embraced American ideals after 1945)web.mit.edu to Takeuchi Yoshimi’s call to remember the defeated spiriteaa.c.u-tokyo.ac.jp, and other cultural critiques, we find precedents that treat victory and defeat as contrasting cultural themes. These discussions highlight how the “spirit of defeat” (敗北の精神) was preserved as a form of aesthetic and moral memory, even as the surface ideology shifted to one of triumph and growth. Toshio Matsumoto) all draw on pre-war Japan (Taishō/early Shōwa era) as their setting or inspiration. Despite spanning different media and decades, these works share a striking decadent sensibility – characterized by eroticism intertwined with death, perversion, “rotten beauty” and a surreal blend of sweetness and destruction. This aesthetic lineage can be traced back to Japan’s 1920s–30s ero-guro-nansensu (“erotic, grotesque, nonsense”) cultural movement, a bourgeois subculture devoted to the deviant and bizarre, described as “a skittish, sometimes nihilistic hedonism that brings Weimar Berlin to mind”en.wikipedia.org. Below, we explore critical observations that discuss the common decadent ethos and narrative style of these works, and consider why they (or their source material) reemerged in the late 20th century, often in the context of Japanese subculture criticism. Shared “Decadent” Themes & Style Several critics and commentators note that these four works, though different in form, rhyme with each other in their themes and tone: Eroticism and Death (Eros & Thanatos): All four narratives blur the line between sexual ecstasy and mortality. Critic Mark Schilling points out that Ishii’s Nejishiki (adapted from Yoshiharu Tsuge’s avant-garde manga) is an “offbeat tale of a struggling cartoonist’s journey between dream and reality, Eros and Thanatos”fareastfilm.com. In this world, sex and death are inextricably linked – for example, a failed suicide and frantic sexual encounters are portrayed with absurd, black humorfareastfilm.com. Similarly, Ōshima’s Ai no Korīda famously depicts an obsessive love affair in 1936 ending in eroticized death (the real-life Sada Abe incident). Ōshima frames the lovers’ private world of incessant lovemaking as “pure” in contrast to the “filthy machinations” of the militarist society around themcriterion.com. The ultimate act of love is also an act of violence – a decadent culmination of Eros in Thanatos. This pattern – sexual climax entwined with destruction – recurs across the works. Grotesque Beauty and Surreal Perversion: Each work cultivates an atmosphere of dreamlike, “rotten” beauty where conventional morals are inverted. In Nejishiki, Ishii uses imagery of “writhing semi-naked flesh” inspired by Hieronymus Bosch in the opening, plunging the viewer into a nightmarish fever-dreamfareastfilm.com. Dogura Magura, adapted from Yumeno Kyūsaku’s 1935 novel, is deliberately hallucinatory and carnivalesque – a “vexing” puzzle full of mad doctors, murder, and insanity. Viewers are “bombarded with an endless array of eccentric characters that are as grotesque as they are mesmerizing,” giving the film a bizarre carnival of decadence tonepostmodernpelican.com. The grotesque is made enticing. Similarly, Hakuchi presents an alternate WWII-ravaged Japan where a fascist media station and a decadent cabaret idol dominate the landscape; one reviewer notes the film’s “extravagance of decadence” in its visuals – from brainwashing propaganda to the imperious chanteuse – which creates an almost baroque satire of mass medialetterboxd.com. In all these works, perverse or abnormal sexuality, mental derangement, and gore (explicit or implicit) are depicted with a peculiar aesthetic polish – an “erotic and grotesque” style that is disturbing yet darkly beautiful. Indeed, Tsuge and Ishii are explicitly called “veteran representatives of the ero-guro (erotic grotesque) side of Japanese popular art”fareastfilm.com, a label equally applicable to the others given their content. “Sweetness and Death” – Innocence Meets Corruption: Another shared motif is the juxtaposition of innocence or tenderness with depravity and doom. Ai no Korīda portrays moments of genuine intimacy and almost childlike playfulness between Sada and Kichi, even as their acts grow more extreme and nihilistic – a coexistence of gentle love and lethal obsession. Hakuchi’s storyline (based on Ango Sakaguchi’s novel) centers on “pure love and the rebirth of the soul” between a naive young man and a simple-minded woman in a ruinous wartime citykinejun.com, yet this sweetness unfolds in a backdrop of surreal war propaganda, decadently costumed performers, and societal collapse – innocence literally sheltering in decay. Dogura Magura, for all its insanity, is framed as a detective story of the mind where a confused young amnesiac tries to grasp truth; amid the grotesquery there is an underlying yearning for identity and love (twisted as it may be). Critics have noted that Ishii’s Nejishiki, despite its twisted imagery, has an “underlying tone” that is “gently tolerant, not mocking… sympathetic”, seeking “wellsprings of our common humanity in… the darkest hours”fareastfilm.comfareastfilm.com. In other words, these works often find a strange poignancy within the perversion – a mix of “甘美 (sweetness)” and death that defines their decadent aesthetic of sublime corruption. Critical Commentary and Analysis A number of critics, scholars, and reviewers have drawn connections among these works or analyzed their stylistic ethos: Ero-Guro Tradition in Film: The influence of Japan’s prewar ero-guro tradition on late 20th-century cinema has been frequently noted. Mark Schilling, for instance, emphasizes how Nejishiki brought together Tsuge and Ishii, two figures steeped in ero-guro sensibilitiesfareastfilm.com. Ishii had long been known for 1960s exploitation films (e.g. Horrors of Malformed Men) that directly adapted Edogawa Rampo and other ero-guro authorsen.wikipedia.org. With Nejishiki, he returned to that decadent genre in the 1990s, visualizing Tsuge’s surreal tale of sexual injury and delirium. Critics hailed the result as a striking continuation of the erotic-grotesque art lineage – a film where “sex becomes a frantically absurd assault across the gulf that separates the sexes,” and a bungled suicide attempt turns into “blackly comic” farcefareastfilm.com. This mirrors the tone of Dogura Magura as well, which reviewers have called “a truly enigmatic psychological drama” that “needs to be seen to be believed”filmhounds.co.uk. The Postmodern Pelican review of Dogura Magura observes how Matsumoto’s film adaptation feels like an “elaborate piece of theatre”, embracing the deviance and controversial ideas of the source novel and translating them into an oneiric, grotesque cinematic experiencepostmodernpelican.compostmodernpelican.com. The film’s “carnivalesque” array of grotesques is not just for shock value, but, as the reviewer notes, serves a “satirical” purpose – Dogura Magura becomes a dark carnival satirizing the boundary between sanity and madnesspostmodernpelican.com. Ōshima’s Decadent Realism: In contrast to the more surrealist works, Ōshima’s In the Realm of the Senses (Ai no Korīda) has been analyzed through political and aesthetic lenses. While not usually labeled “ero-guro,” it shares the decadent core: explicit eroticism pushed to fatal extremes. Film scholar Donald Richie explains that Ōshima did not intend it as pornography but as a taboo-breaking art film using real sex to “explor[e] and demystify Japanese culture”criterion.com. Set in 1936 on the eve of fascism, the film pointedly isolates the lovers from the outside world (where war and repression loom). Richie notes that the historical context (the February 26 coup, rising militarism) serves as a backdrop that heightens the couple’s retreat into an amoral erotic Edencriterion.com. The film thus “encouraged empathy” for two people who create their own world of pleasure in defiance of societycriterion.com. Critic Tony Rayns and others have remarked on this counterpoint of purity and corruption – the lovers’ indulgence is portrayed almost tenderly, implicitly criticizing the true perversity of the militarist authority around themcriterion.comcriterion.com. In Ai no Korīda, decadence becomes a form of resistance: Sada and Kichi’s “immoral” act of erotic asphyxiation and mutilation is in its own context elevated to a kind of sacred union, untainted by the “filth” of external moral hypocrisycriterion.com. This inversion – crime as purity, normativity as corruption – is a classic Decadent (with a capital D) trope, linking Ōshima’s film to the same anti-establishment erotic nihilism that runs through Hakuchi or Dogura Magura. Makoto Tezuka’s Hakuchi and Ango’s Legacy: Hakuchi, though a later and less internationally known film, has attracted commentary for its ambitious blend of literary decadence and pop-culture satire. Based on Ango Sakaguchi’s 1946 novella (Ango was a famous burai-ha decadence author), Tezuka’s film transposes Ango’s postwar “fallen world” vision into a stylized alternate 1990s where World War II never ended. Reviews often highlight the film’s lavish, decayed mise-en-scène: the bombed-out city dominated by a gleaming Media Station, the grotesque pageantry of a fascistic pop idol (played by actress Reika Hashimoto) and her rapturous fans, and the jarring mix of high-tech (CGI effects) with retro aesthetics. One reviewer describes it as a “retro-futuristic dystopia” and notes “the extravagance of decadence encapsulated by the brainwashing TV news/shows and the peculiar and imperious singer Ginga,” which together form “an effective satirical critique of media and stars in general”letterboxd.com. In other words, Hakuchi uses over-the-top decadent imagery (decaying cityscapes, eroticized media spectacle, decadent costumes and makeup) purposefully to comment on the seductive toxicity of mass media and authoritarianism. This approach resonates with Ango’s original message in essays like Darakuron (On Decadence) – that indulging in “decadence” was a way to strip falsehood and confront reality after the war. Critics have noted that Tezuka, who spent 10 years developing this filmasianmoviepulse.com, was not initially an Ango devoteenote.com but found in Hakuchi’s fantasy the means to explore late-20th-century anxieties through a blend of nostalgic decadence and postmodern sensibility. The result is often compared to German Expressionist cinema and the Angura (underground) theater style – a true cross-temporal homage to Japan’s culture of ero, guro, nansensu. 20th-Century Revival and Subculture Context It is no coincidence that these decadence-themed stories (many originally from the 1920s–40s) reappeared in Japanese visual culture around the 1970s–1990s. Critics and cultural historians tie this trend to broader postwar and postmodern currents: Postwar Avant-Garde and Censorship Battles: Ōshima’s Ai no Korīda in 1976 came amid a wave of 1960s–70s Japanese New Wave and Art Theater Guild films that challenged taboos. In the context of a rapidly modernizing but socially conservative Japan, reviving a notorious prewar erotic crime was a provocative statement. Ōshima had to shoot the film in France to escape Japanese censorship, and he later faced obscenity charges for the screenplay publicationcriterion.com. His insistence on showing unvarnished erotic extremity can be seen as a radical continuation of the prewar ero-guro spirit, which had been suppressed under wartime and Occupation censorship. As Richie notes, Ōshima “created a style that showed everything and at the same time encouraged empathy”, aiming for “a politicized eroticism rather than a pornographic performance”criterion.com. Thus, Ai no Korīda helped reopen the door for treating “decadent” themes as serious art, paving the way for later filmmakers to openly explore Japan’s dark, erotic past. *The 1990s “Kichiku” Boom – Rediscovering Decadence: In Japan’s 1990s subculture, there was an explicit revival of interest in “bad taste” and dark subcultures labeled kichiku-kei (literally “brutal” or “demonic” style). Commentators like Yoshiharu Tokuda note that “90s subculture had a subgenre called ‘kichiku-kei’,” which “deliberately presented immoral, outrageous expressions” and courted scandalimidas.jpimidas.jp. This trend spanned extreme fetish magazines, underground music, and literature reprints. Notably, it involved a nostalgia for the ero-guro-nansensu era – the phrase itself enjoyed renewed popularity in the 90sja.wikipedia.org. Publications like 危ない1号 (Abunai Ichigō) magazine unabashedly covered topics like drugs, bizarre crimes, scatology, and also “introduced cult films and heterodox literature and thought”, shining light on “‘yabai’ (dangerous) works” from the pastimidas.jp. In this environment, adapting classic decadent novels to film made sense as part of the retro-trend. Dogura Magura’s 1988 film release, for example, came just as the “underground occult boom” of the 80s (e.g. interest in Taishō-era curiosities, the success of Teito Monogatari, etc.) was paving the way for 90s extremity. By the late 90s, filmmakers like Teruo Ishii and Makoto Tezuka could secure interest (if not huge budgets) for projects reviving prewar grotesque-romance tales. Ishii’s Nejishiki was essentially an independent comeback film by a 73-year-old director, embraced by cult cinema fans rather than mainstream studiosfareastfilm.comfareastfilm.com. Tezuka’s Hakuchi similarly was a passion project that won a digital award at the Venice Film Festivalasianmoviepulse.com but was niche in domestic box office. The timing of these films’ releases corresponds with Japan’s “Lost Decade” of economic stagnation and social malaise. Some critics have conjectured that the disillusionment of the 90s, post-bubble era — marked by a sense of societal decay, a reaction against the shiny consumerism of the 80s, and crises like the Aum cult incident — made the decadent visions of Japan’s past suddenly resonant. The fin-de-siècle atmosphere (approaching the year 2000) perhaps fueled fascination with early Shōwa’s own turbulent descent into darkness. By resurrecting the imagery of a decaying imperial Japan (bomb-ravaged cities, asylums, erotic murder, etc.), these works implicitly comment on late-20th-century Japan’s moral and spiritual exhaustion. In short, the reappearance of these “decadent” works at century’s end has been read as both a subcultural trend – a nod to an underground canon of Japanese decadence – and a reflection of contemporary anxieties, using past decadence as an allegory for present issues. Conclusion Across Ai no Korīda, Nejishiki, Hakuchi, and Dogura Magura, one finds a common aesthetic of decay and taboo – a world of erotic obsession, grotesque imagery, and a blurring of pleasure with death. Critics in film and literary circles have indeed discussed this shared “腐敗美” (corrupt beauty) and “退廃的演出” (decadent staging) in various ways: some situate it in the long tradition of ero-guro culturefareastfilm.com, others analyze its political subtexts (e.g. anti-authority or media critique)criterion.comletterboxd.com, and many simply celebrate the bold transgression of norms it represents. Importantly, these works were (re)made in the late 20th century when Japanese subculture was actively reclaiming prewar avant-garde sensibilities – there was a newfound appreciation for the “twisted brilliance” of these storiesfareastfilm.com, no longer seen as mere shock schlock but as carrying a distinctive postmodern artistry. Whether in academic film journals or underground zines, one finds commentary recognizing that in these films “eroticism and grotesquerie” are not gratuitous, but rather a deliberate style that confronts viewers with ideas of freedom, identity, and the absurdity of social constraints. In summary, there are critical writings (both in Japanese and English, in subculture critiques and film analyses) that draw links among these works – often underscoring their shared decadent ethos and exploring how, by reviving early Shōwa’s “sweet-and-deadly” aesthetic at the century’s end, these creators reflected on the timeless lure of decadence in Japanese culture. The end-of-20th-century revival of such tainted romances invites us to ask, as these critics do, what void in the modern psyche they fill – be it a form of resistance, nostalgia, or a dark mirror to the age’s soul. Reality of Settlement – Between Eros and Thanatos: The lived experiences of women in Manchukuo were often harshly at odds with the dream. Primary sources – diaries, memoirs, oral histories – reveal how women’s bodies became battlegrounds between nurturing life and witnessing death. Many women did fulfill roles as wives and mothers on the colonial frontier, thus enacting the “Eros” function of giving life: they married fellow settlers (sometimes through arranged introductions upon arrival) and bore children in Manchuria. For instance, poet Kikue Idutsu, a woman who emigrated as a bride in 1943, later recorded her experiences in haiku and tanka, poignantly encapsulating the mix of hope and sorrow in her life abroadja.wikipedia.org. Idutsu’s artful memoirs (and those of others like Nishizaki Kiku, a female aviator-turned-settlerja.wikipedia.org) provide a window into the emotional world of these women – the initial romance of adventure and marriage, followed by the struggle to raise families amid an increasingly untenable war situation. However, as the war turned against Japan, Thanatos (death and decay) came to eclipse Eros in the Manchurian dream. By 1944-45, many male settlers were conscripted into the failing military, leaving communities of mostly women, children, and elderly exposed to violence and deprivationapjjf.orgapjjf.org. Soviet invasion in August 1945 and the subsequent chaos triggered nightmares that belied the utopian rhetoric. A particularly tragic example is the Kurokawa Settler Community case. In this community of 600 settlers, when faced with invasion and the collapse of order, the leaders “dispatched young unmarried women to provide sexual services to Soviet Red Army soldiers in hope of assuring the survival of the local community”apjjf.org. In effect, the community sacrificed the erotic and bodily autonomy of its girls to appease the conquerors and stave off massacre. Dozens of teenage women were thus offered to enemy soldiers – a horrific perversion of their role from life-givers to objects of barter to fend off death. Many of these women endured rape and trauma; their ordeal was long kept silent as an unspeakable sacrifice for the group. This extreme incident underscores how the female body became the last currency of a “rotting” utopia, used in a fatalistic bid to preserve life (Eros) at the cost of honor and well-being (a form of living death, Thanatos). Even aside from such extreme cases, countless women suffered violence during the chaotic repatriation: rapes, abductions, starvation, and separation from children were common themes in testimoniesapjjf.orgapjjf.org. Many mothers had to watch infants and loved ones die during the arduous trek to safety, experiences later recounted with tremendous pain in their memoirs. The dream of Manchukuo, “sweet” in propaganda, thus ended in the “decay” of starvation, loss, and death for these women, forcing them to confront the full span of Eros and Thanatos. Motherhood, Submission, and the Nation-State: The relationship between women (as bearers of children) and the wartime state was fundamentally one of ideological control over the female body. Scholars note that Japan’s militarist regime aggressively promoted motherhood as a patriotic duty. The slogan “Umeyo, Fuyaseyo!” (“Breed and multiply!”) encapsulates how the government exhorted women to have more children for the empire’s expansion. In Manchukuo, this took on a colonial dimension: Japanese women were expected not only to boost population numbers but also to raise “strong” sons and daughters to populate and hold Japan’s imperial frontiers. The state’s gender ideology was often summarized by the phrase “良妻賢母” (ryōsai kenbo, “Good Wife, Wise Mother”), which idealized women who subordinated themselves – as obedient wives to their husbands and selfless mothers to their children – for the sake of family and nation. Historians Wenwen Wang and others have shown that in Manchukuo the Japanese authorities explicitly tied this conservative ethos to colonial goals: schools for settler girls taught that being a virtuous, industrious wife and mother was how women could “serve the state”thechinastory.org. Thus, submission was ingrained as a feminine virtue – submission to one’s husband, to maternal duty, and to the imperial cause. Resistance to these roles was generally not tolerated; women who joined the migration were under heavy social pressure to marry quickly (if single) and to bear children. In one case, a 17-year-old volunteer named Sadako was pushed into marriage in Manchuria despite her own misgivings, because the program organizers expected all female volunteers to become settlers’ wivesapjjf.org. Once married and pregnant, a woman was further bound by both personal commitment and state expectation. Nation and motherhood became fused: the children a woman bore were literally the future “children of the empire.” Critics have pointed out that this state appropriation of women’s reproductive capacity shares features with contemporary fascist regimes’ policies. Chizuko Ueno, a prominent Japanese feminist, argued that the wartime Japanese state effectively nationalized women’s wombs – valuing women primarily for their ability to give birth to (and sacrifice for) the next generation of imperial subjects. Women’s sexuality was bifurcated into sanctioned and unsanctioned forms: the “good mothers” who produced soldiers and colonists were lauded, while other women, such as the “comfort women” (forced prostitutes for the military), were exploited in darker ways – yet both were outcomes of a system that saw female bodies as tools for male and state agendas. In Manchukuo’s context, even the idealized mothers ultimately faced powerlessness: as the empire crumbled, their maternal duty to protect children met cruel reality – e.g. some settler mothers had to contemplate infanticide or suicide to avoid worse fates at enemy hands, and many were separated from their children (the so-called zanryū fujin and zanryū koji, the stranded wives and orphaned children left in China after Japan’s surrender)apjjf.orgapjjf.org. In these extreme circumstances, the obedience and self-sacrifice drilled into women became a tragic last resort – they complied even with directives to give themselves or their children up, reflecting how thoroughly the ethos of submission had been internalized. Feminist Reflections – “Birthing” and the Cycle of Life and Death: Thinkers like Julia Kristeva have provided a philosophical lens to understand the profound psychic tension in motherhood – a tension very relevant to the Manchurian women’s experience. Kristeva describes motherhood as a state of “continuous separation” – the mother’s body is literally split to create new life, and with the birth of the child she suffers a loss of a part of herselfpublicseminar.org. In her essay “Stabat Mater” (1977), Kristeva famously wrote, “A mother is a continuous separation, a division of the very flesh…an eternal loss”. She portrays the mother as a figure who must endure pain and self-mourning (Thanatos) even as she gives love and life (Eros). The mother’s identity in patriarchal cultures, Kristeva argues, is often silenced – she exists socially only as a mother, through “milk and tears” rather than an active voicepublicseminar.orgpublicseminar.org. This insight resonates strongly with the Japanese women’s plight: they were valued for milk (nurturing new life) and ultimately left with tears (mourning dead loved ones and lost homes). Kristeva’s vision of the Virgin Mary weeping at the foot of her crucified son – “a part of her-self die” – became a reality for many Manchurian mothers who saw sons sent to war or lost children to famine and violencepublicseminar.org. The collision of Eros and Thanatos is inherent in motherhood, and in Manchukuo it was tragically accelerated by history. Japanese feminist theory also critiques how the state exploited this maternal self-sacrifice. Ueno Chizuko has noted that nationalism often traps women in a paradox: they are exalted as “mothers of the nation” but thereby robbed of personal agency and drawn into glorifying their own suffering. In wartime Japan, motherhood was idealized in rhetoric but entailed stoic endurance of loss – a dynamic of “beautiful sacrifice” that Ueno and others see as foundational to nationalist propaganda. Philosopher Tetsu Ukai has likewise examined how fascist ideologies link “birth” and “death” in a twisted way – encouraging women to give birth for the nation, even if it means those children (and potentially the women themselves) will be expended in war. This is essentially a biopolitical critique: the state turns women’s biological capacity into a means of production for its utopian ambitions, with little regard for the individual woman’s well-being. The Eros of creating life is co-opted into the service of Thanatos, the machinery of war and domination. From a “Sweet, Decayed Dream” to Utopian Critique: Analyzing Manchukuo through these lenses of language and gender reveals how utopian visions can mask dystopian practices. The “sweet dream” of a continental paradise was preserved in lofty language and imagery – a narrative of fertile lands, happy families, and harmonious colonial coexistence. Women, seduced by this dream, participated as agents of hope, literally carrying the future through pregnancy and hard work. Yet, the very same language that inspired them also demanded their absolute submission. When the dream “rotted” in the crucible of war, the beautiful words gave way to commands and coercion: endure, obey, sacrifice. The female perspective uncovers this harsh truth: the promise of utopia often feeds on the bodies of the subaltern, be it women, the colonized, or other marginalized groups. Understanding how language (as propaganda and ideology) intertwined with the body (reproductive and sexual labor) in Manchukuo provides a valuable framework for contemporary critique of utopias. It shows that whenever a political project paints a utopian future that requires people to surrender their agency or bodily autonomy for the greater good, one should be wary of the potential for exploitation. The women of Manchukuo lived through a cycle where language shaped their perception of the world, guided them into certain embodied roles, and ultimately was used to justify extreme violations of those very bodies. Their history, illuminated by cross-disciplinary research and their own testimonies, stands as a cautionary tale. In current times, feminist scholars and critics of nationalist or utopian movements draw on such lessons to ask: Who is being asked to bear the cost of this “dream”? Whether analyzing pronatalist policies, nationalist motherhood ideals, or any grand narrative that elevates an ideal over individual lives, the experience of Manchukuo’s women urges us to look skeptically at the rhetoric of utopia. By bringing to light the mechanisms of language and power over the body, these studies help us connect the dots from a forgotten corner of 1940s Asia to universal patterns of domination – thereby grounding any modern utopia critique in the very real, lived consequences of past “sweet dreams” turned sour.
